Grammar Lessons for ESL Teachers

Photo from Poodar Chu, a student in Guangzhou. (Unsplash)

If there ever were a losing battle, it would surely be teaching English grammar to unfluent speakers using English alone. Grammar is too nuanced, and the grammatical lexicon too esoteric, for lessons delivered wholly in the target language to be efficient for most classes. If you are hired to teach grammar, you would be well advised to translate all grammatical jargon into the students' native tongue, or structure your lessons to catch only the most frequent grammatical interferences from the students' native language. (See "Learner English," by Swan and Smith for details about the latter.)

You may, however, have an advanced and dedicated group of English learners, or (more realistically) have to make do to teach the assigned curricula to less advanced students. When I found myself in both situations between my different classes within a single semester, I decided to make Powerpoint presentations that would introduce grammatical principles at varying levels of depth. In the examples below, you will find relatively simple slides with less jargon, and also more detailed slides that capture the intricacies of English grammar. They can be used at the teacher's discretion to aid students in their fluency.

My presentations utilize the Interchange series of books by Jack C Richards. Following the books' structure, my presentations included:
    • infinitives and gerunds for uses and purposes
    • imperatives and infinitives for giving suggestions
    • relative clauses of time
    • adverbial clauses of time
    • time contrasts
    • conditional sentences with "if" clauses
    • gerunds
    • short responses
    • clauses with "because"
    • passive with "by" (simple past)
    • passive without "by" (simple present)
    • past continuous vs. simple past
    • present perfect continuous
    • participles as adjectives
    • relative clauses
    • modals and adverbs
    • permission, obligation, and prohibition
    • unreal conditional sentences with "if" clauses
    • past modals
    • reported speech: requests
    • reported speech: statements
    • relative pronouns as subjects and objects
    • clauses with it + adverbial clauses with when
    • gerund phrases as subjects and objects
    • comparisons with adjectives, verbs, nouns, and past participles
    • requests with modals, if clauses, and gerunds
    • indirect requests
    • past continuous vc. Simple past
    • past perfect
    • noun phrases containing relative clauses
    • expectations: the custom to, (not) supposed to, expected to, (not) acceptable to
    • describing problems with past participles as adjectives and with nouns
    • describing problems with heep + gerund, need + grund, and need + passive infinitive
    • passive in the presente continuous and presente perfect
    • prepositions of cause; infinitive clauses and phrases
    • would rather and would prefer
    • by + gerund to describe how to do things
    • have or get something done (active and passive)
    • making suggestions with gerunds, infinitives, modals + verbs, and negative questions
    • referring to time in the past with adverbs and prepositions
    • predicting the future with will, future continuous, and future perfect
    • time clauses
    • describing regrets and hypothetical situations with should no have + past participle and if clauses + past perfect
    • describing purpose with infinitive clauses and infinitive clauses with for
    • giving reasons
    • past modals for degrees of certainty
    • past modals for opinions and advice
    • the passive to describe process with is/are + past participle and modal + be + past participle
    • defining and nondefining relative clauses
    • giving recommendations and opinions with passive modals
    • tag questions for opinions
    • complex noun phrases containing gerunds
    • accomplishments with the presente perfect and simple past
    • goals with the future perfect and would like to have + past participle
