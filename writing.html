While at Teach For America, I wrote a weekly newsletter for roughly 80 new teachers who would be leading Philadelphia students the following autumn. Each newsletter began with a short piece to keep readers updated and motivated on their paths to the classroom. I've excerpted some of these pieces below.

(Flickr)

Brotherly Love

Last week, our humble Philly office had some guests. They were a team of TFA staff visiting from around the country to discuss our impact in local communities. So, we showed them our classrooms and introduced them to our students. We talked about race, poverty, politics, vision, and strategy. We discussed our successes and our, ahem, less-than-successes. In return, they explained how our endeavors in Philly relate to those of TFA staff and corps members across the country.

It was fitting to have such a get together in the city of brotherly love. We here in the office call ourselves the “Philly Pham,” but we sometimes forget we’re part of a larger tribe, with our TFA kinfolk doing the same work across America. Some of us are in our 20s and others are nearing retirement, but we’re all linked in our wholehearted commitment to the same cause.

And that brings me to the “love” bit of “brotherly love.” One visitor last week was a founding Philly corps member from 2003. Holding back tears, he spoke of the kids he taught and the desire he had to always do more. Those experiences are why, he said, he’s on staff at TFA today – to continue making a difference. It was hard to miss the love in his voice.

But love takes work, and there’s a heck of a lot of work to be done in our schools. While you won’t single-handedly resolve the systemic issues depriving Philly’s kids of an equitable education, one day you can say you were part of the movement in this country that did.

Uncomfortable Questions

What do you think when you hear “light skin” vs. “dark skin”? Is “you’re pretty for a dark-skin girl” considered a compliment? Are you comfortable with your skin color?  

And are you comfortable with these questions?

Well, Philly students are. Last week, local high school students released a video titled, “For the Love of Brown Girls.” In a mere 10 minutes, these teenagers cover more ground than some classes do in an entire semester. Colorism, racism, misogyny, intersectionality, implicit bias, the influence of mass media - they press the topics that even some adults can’t handle. They don’t always have the answers, but there’s no shortage of questions:

Where did this even come from?
How do you learn how not to love yourself?
Or, how do you learn how to not love yourself?
How do you learn to put down your own kin?
How to put down your own race?
How to put down the woman who looks closest like you,
who’ll make the most beautiful babies with you,
who is always gonna root for you for your entire life?  

If prejudice can be learned, then it can be unlearned. If hate can be learned, then so can love. In a city that needs all the love it can get, we lead for the love of our students. That means asking the uncomfortable questions, hearing the difficult answers, and always bringing something to the (classroom) table. Is it challenging? Of course. Should we as leaders fear challenges? Hell no.

The Heritage City

The city of Philadelphia has a lot to celebrate from its past. Founded in 1683, Philly today still retains most of its original street & city plan. In the 1700s, Independence Hall birthed the Declaration of Independence and United States Constitution. Philly was the capital of government and finance in the 18th century, an industrial powerhouse in the 19th century, and a commercial & cultural hub in the 20th. According to the OWHC, it was these achievements that led to Philadelphia being named the USA’s first World Heritage City last year.

But you’ll notice something missing from the above account. As it currently stands, Philly is 44% Black / African-American. Its history, then, is largely African-American history. Last week, we in the Philly Pham had an all-staff session to probe our collective knowledge of this rich past. We knew that Philly residents attend influential black churches and some of the most important HBCUs. Billie Holiday, Patti LaBelle, and Questlove were born in the city. The Black Panther Party held a convention at Temple University, Mumia Abu-Jamal was tried at City Hall, and the headquarters of the radical group MOVE was literally bombed by the police on Osage Avenue.

It’s a lot to cover in a mere month – even with the extra day. There’s no way such a limited amount of time can capture the entirety of Philly residents’ stories & achievements. So as February comes to a close, I ask you to commit to extending the celebration of black history throughout the year, throughout your lessons as a teacher, and, if all goes according to plan, throughout your students’ lives.
