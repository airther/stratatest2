Grammar Lessons for ESL Teachers

Photo from Poodar Chu, a student in Guangzhou. (Unsplash)

If there ever were a losing battle, it would surely be teaching the finer points of English grammar *in English* to inexperienced speakers. Grammar is too nuanced, and the grammatical lexicon too esoteric, for such lessons delivered in the target language to be efficient for most classes. If you are hired to teach grammar, you would be well advised to teach in the students' native tongue, or, failing that, aim to catch the most common grammatical interferences from the students' native language. (See " Learner English," by Swan and Smith for details about the latter.)

You may, however, be lucky enough to have an advanced and dedicated group of English learners, or (more realistically) have to make do to teach the assigned curricula to less advanced learners. When I found myself in both of these positions with different classes during the same semester, I decided to make Powerpoint presentations that would introduce grammatical principles at varying levels of depth. In the examples below, you will find relatively simple slides with less jargon, and more detailed slides that capture the intricacies of English grammar.



My presentations utilize the Interchange series of books by Jack C Richards. Following the books' structure, my presentations included:
    • infinitives and gerunds for uses and purposes
    • imperatives and infinitives for giving suggestions
    • relative clauses of time
    • adverbial clauses of time
    • time contrasts
    • conditional sentences with if clauses
    • gerunds
    • short responses
    • clauses with because
    • passive with by (simple past)
    • passive without by (simple presente)
    • past continuous vs. simple past
    • presente perfect continuous
    • participles as adjectives
    • relative clauses
    • modals and adverbs
    • permission, obligation, and prohibition
    • unreal conditional sentences with if clauses
    • past modals
    • reported speech: requests
    • reported speech: statements
