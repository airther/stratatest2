ESL Materials for Teaching Chinese Students

Photo from Poodar Chu, a student in Guangzhou. (Unsplash)

As an ESL teacher in China, a colleague of mine asked rhetorically, "What can we (as native speakers) provide our students that their English books and local teachers cannot?" His conclusion was that, native speakers are especially well positioned to catch and correct errors in pronunciation, in a way that Chinese teachers (who had also learned English as a second language) and inert textbooks could not. Intrigued by this observation, I researched the frequency and causes of mispronunciation by Chinese students.

One of the primary factors, I found, is that Chinese dialects do not contain all of the phonemes present in English, such that students will substitute sounds from their native language for the intended English phoneme. (This is an example of what is often called "native language interference.") Worse yet, even some of the phonemes that are shared with by thr native dialect are misapplied by English learners due to aural or orthographic misunderstandings. A student may hear or read an English sound as one phoneme when it is actually supposed to be another, even though both sounds are present in their native language.

I decided to formulate a multi-pronged approach to help students better understand and produce phonetic distinctions by using minimal pairs, an explicit understanding of physical articulation, and other techniques. I used dialogs, a popular ESL teaching device, to frame these pronunciation lessons and to provide conversation starters for class discussion (which seasoned ESL teachers will recognize as one of the more engaging parts of class for many students). I have shared these dialogs and supporting materials below as a starting point for other ESL teachers looking to build pronunciation lessons for their own students.

Some of my inspirations for the dialogs below include:
    • Learner English, Swan and Smith
    • Whaddaya Say, Nina Weinstein
    • Understanding Pronunciation Variations Facing ESL Students, Liang-Chen Lin
    • English Pronunciation/Listening, Okanagan College
    • Chinese Phonotactic Patterns and the Pronunciation Difficulties Of Mandarin-Speaking EFL Learners, Hui-Ling Huang & James Radant

Hugged or Kissed?

Dialog

A I heard that you kissed him.
B I didn’t kiss him! He only hugged me.
A Oh! In other words, you acted like friends!

Pronunciation: "-ed" Suffix

Last Sound Before "-ed" "-ed" Pronunciation Example Notes
"t" or "d" /Id/ (or /əd/) Acted -
Voiced /d/ Hugged Voiced endings include: b, g, th, v, z, zh, j, m, n, ng, r, l, and vowels
Voiceless /t/ Kissed Voiceless endings include p, k, th, f, s, sh, and ch

    • Pronunciation: Connected Speech

    The final "-ed" sound can be connected to the next word in the sentence without pause if the next word begins with a vowel sound. This can also be done with pronouns or helping verbs that beging with "h", such as:
  • he
  • his
  • her
  • him
  • has
  • have
    • This is called h-deletion. Below are some examples of connected speech using h-deletion:

    Vocab

    "In other words": said or understood another way; [Introduces an explanation, simplification, or clarification].

    Make Your Own Dialog

    Two people talk about their friend (Person 3) who moved to another city.
    Person 1: Liked Person 3 (as a friend).
    Person 2: Loved Person 3 (more than a friend).
    Remember: use he/him/her to talk about Person 3.

    Discussion: Friendship and Sex/Gender

    1. Are male and female friendships different? How? What do they do together?
    2. Are male-female friendships as close as same-sex friendships? Why?
    3. Some people think men and women can’t be “just friends.” Why?

    Lots of Parties

    Dialog

    A There are lots of parties this Thursday.
    B I’m going to Liz’s. She’s a friend of mine.
    A Go ahead, but don’t drink a lot of beer.

    Pronunciation: The "-s" Inflectional Ending

    The "-s" inflectional ending is used plural nouns, possessives, or the third person singular of present tense verbs.

    Last Sound Before "-s" ending "-s" Pronunciation Example Notes
    Voiceless "s" Maps Voiceless endings include: t, p, k, th, f
    Voiced "z" Dogs Voiced endings include: d, b, g, th, v, m, n, ng, r, l, and vowels
    Sibilant "iz" Horses Sibilants are the "hiss and buzz" sounds: s, z, ʃ, ʒ, tʃ, dʒ

    • Pronunciation: Relaxed Speech

    If the word "of" occurs before a word beginning with a consonant sound, it can be pronounced /ə/. However, this is not true if the word "of" begins a sentence, or is stressed by the speaker.

    Text Without Relaxed Speech Phonetic Sound with Relaxed Speech
    A cup of coffee A cup /ə/ coffee
    Of course [no change]

    Vocab

    "Go Ahead": to proceed; to begin.

    Make Your Own Dialog

    Two college roommates talk about having a party in their room.
    Person 1: Wants to have a party.
    Person 2: Doesn't want to have a party.

    Discussion: Parties and College

    1. When do Chinese college students have parties? Where are the parties?
    2. What do Chinese college students do at parties?
    3. Do you like to drink alcohol? Why or why not?
